A social-ecological view of adolescent well-being, with social cognition as a key mechanism linking physical, digital, and AI-mediated environments with individual functioning.

Adolescent mental health is not only about reducing distress. It is also about understanding the capacities, relationships, identities, and environments that allow young people to function well.

Research position

Beyond the disease model.

Much adolescent mental health research begins with what goes wrong. My work begins with a broader question: what does it mean for adolescents to feel well, function well, relate well, and develop well?

Flourishing in Context is the conceptual frame for this work. It treats adolescent mental health as a developmental and social phenomenon, not only as a private individual trait or the inverse of distress.

Three pillars

What I study

My research sits at the intersection of positive mental health, social context, and social cognition.

Positive functioning

Positive mental health

Well-being, flourishing, resilience, self-esteem, emotional functioning, positive self-image, and psychological strengths in adolescence.

Ecological context

Social environments

Families, peers, schools, socioeconomic context, digital and AI-mediated environments, social norms, and cultural expectations.

Mechanisms

Social cognition

Self-perception, perceived peer norms, social comparison, intersubjectivity, identity, status beliefs, and meaning-making.

Current directions

Research directions

These lines of work connect developmental psychology, social psychology, psychometrics, and adolescent mental health research.

Systems

Positive mental health systems

Mapping how strengths, well-being, stress, social relationships, and symptoms organize as systems in adolescence.

Resilience

Resilience across cohorts and contexts

Studying whether adolescent resilience has stable core features, and which features shift across developmental stage and historical context.

Norms

Self, peers, and perceived norms

Examining how adolescents compare themselves with the average peer, and how these perceived gaps relate to well-being and distress.

Digital and AI life

Identity in hybrid and AI-mediated environments

Understanding adolescent identity formation in a world where online, offline, and AI-mediated social life are increasingly connected.

Why this matters

Context is not background.

Adolescence is a period of rapid psychological, social, and identity development. Young people become especially sensitive to belonging, status, comparison, peer influence, and social evaluation.

Understanding adolescent mental health therefore requires more than identifying individual vulnerability. It requires studying the social conditions and social-cognitive mechanisms through which young people come to understand themselves and their futures.
