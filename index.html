Mental health research has largely asked what goes wrong. My work asks what helps adolescents flourish.

I study how social contexts, from families and schools to digital and AI-mediated environments, shape positive mental health in adolescence, drawing on large-scale Singaporean adolescent cohorts.

Adolescent Self Family Peers School Digital life AI systems Culture Society Social cognition
Research focus

Three linked questions.

My work asks what positive mental health is, how social worlds shape it, and what social-cognitive mechanisms connect contexts with adolescent functioning.

Positive functioning

What does it mean for adolescents to do well?

Well-being, flourishing, resilience, self-esteem, emotional functioning, positive self-image, and psychological strengths in adolescence.

Ecological context

How do social environments matter?

Families, peers, schools, socioeconomic context, digital and AI-mediated environments, social norms, and cultural expectations.

Mechanisms

How do contexts become psychologically meaningful?

Self-perception, perceived peer norms, social comparison, intersubjectivity, identity, status beliefs, and meaning-making.

Current directions

Ongoing lines of work.

Positive mental health systems

Mapping how strengths, well-being, stress, social relationships, and symptoms organize as systems in adolescence.

Resilience across cohorts and contexts

Studying which features of adolescent resilience remain stable, and which shift across developmental stage and historical context.

Self, peers, and perceived norms

Examining how adolescents compare themselves with the average peer, and how perceived gaps relate to well-being and distress.

Identity in hybrid and AI-mediated environments

Understanding identity formation in a world where online, offline, and AI-mediated social life are increasingly connected.
