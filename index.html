About

Why "Positive" Matters

The World Health Organization defines mental health as a state of well-being in which people realise their potential, cope with the normal stresses of life, and contribute meaningfully to their communities. It is not the absence of illness. Yet most of the research, the funding, and the public conversation treats mental health as if it were simply the opposite of mental illness. That framing has real consequences for how we support young people.

I am a social psychologist. Where much of psychology focuses on the individual, I study the social contexts that surround them. Families, peer groups, schools, communities, and broader cultural structures all shape how adolescents think, feel, and function. This perspective draws on Bronfenbrenner's ecological model, which recognises that human development is always embedded in nested layers of social influence.

What interests me most is the mechanism: how do social environments actually get "under the skin"? My research investigates social cognition as the pathway through which contextual factors translate into individual well-being. Adolescence is a developmental period when young people are particularly responsive to their social worlds, which makes it both a window of vulnerability and a window of opportunity.

My body of work, Flourishing in Context, brings together multiple studies and collaborations toward this aim. Among these, the PoMAd (Positive Mental Health in Adolescents) study was the foundational project that established this line of inquiry, working with large-scale Singaporean adolescent cohort data to build the evidence base for what positive mental health looks like, how it develops, and what sustains it.

Research

What I Study and Why It Matters

Flourishing in Context sits at the intersection of social psychology and developmental science. My work is organised around four interconnected themes, each with direct implications for how society supports young people. Across all of them, I draw on rigorous psychometric methods to measure the constructs and processes I study.

Positive Mental Health Framework

Moving beyond the disease model to study mental health as the presence of well-being, not just the absence of disorder. This includes developing and validating measures that capture what it means for adolescents to flourish, providing tools that researchers and practitioners can use.

→ Enables schools and policymakers to track student well-being, not just screen for problems.

Adolescent Social Realities

Adolescents today inhabit social worlds that are fundamentally different from those their parents, teachers, or policymakers grew up in. Understanding those worlds requires seeing them through young people's eyes. This line of research examines the lived experiences of adolescents and how mental health is framed across the contexts they encounter, from peer conversations and social media to news headlines and parliamentary discourse, to build an evidence base that reflects current realities rather than adult assumptions.

→ Grounds research and policy in what young people actually experience, so interventions address the right problems.

Social Ecology of Adolescent Well-being

Examining how social environments at multiple ecological levels shape mental health outcomes during adolescence: from family dynamics and peer relationships to school climates and societal structures.

→ Shifts intervention targets from "fixing the teenager" to improving the environments they inhabit.

Social Cognition as Mechanism

Investigating how adolescents perceive, interpret, and respond to their social worlds, and how these cognitive processes link social contexts to individual outcomes. Understanding these pathways is essential for designing interventions that work.

→ Identifies specific, actionable targets for programmes that help young people navigate social challenges.
