Research

Research

My work sits at the intersection of social psychology and developmental science. It examines how adolescents develop well-being, resilience, identity, and self-understanding within the social worlds around them.

Across these themes, I draw on psychometric, longitudinal, and systems-oriented methods, grounding the work in large-scale Singaporean adolescent cohorts and in adolescent mental health and well-being as they unfold in Singapore and the wider Asian context.

Positive mental health framework

Moving beyond the disease model to study mental health as the presence of well-being, not just the absence of disorder. This includes well-being, flourishing, emotional functioning, self-esteem, social connectedness, positive self-image, and psychological strengths.

→ Enables schools and policymakers to track adolescent well-being, not just screen for problems.

Adolescent social realities

Adolescents today inhabit social worlds that are fundamentally different from those their parents, teachers, or policymakers grew up in. This line of research examines their lived experiences and how mental health is framed across the contexts they encounter, from social media and AI-mediated environments to public discourse and policy debates.

→ Grounds research and policy in young people's actual social worlds, rather than adult assumptions.

Social ecology of adolescent well-being

Examining how social environments at multiple ecological levels shape mental health outcomes during adolescence, from family dynamics and peer relationships to school climates, digital and AI-mediated environments, cultural expectations, and societal structures.

→ Broadens intervention targets from the individual adolescent to the environments they inhabit.

Social cognition as mechanism

Investigating how adolescents perceive, interpret, and respond to their social worlds, and how these cognitive processes link social contexts to individual outcomes. This includes social comparison, perceived peer norms, self-concept clarity, identity, status beliefs, and intersubjectivity.

→ Identifies social-cognitive mechanisms that can inform more targeted interventions.
