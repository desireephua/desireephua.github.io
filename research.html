Research

Positive mental health, social context, and social cognition.

My research examines how adolescents develop well-being and resilience in relation to family, peers, schools, digital environments, culture, and society.

Theme 1

Positive mental health and flourishing

I study adolescent mental health as more than the absence of symptoms. This includes well-being, flourishing, emotional functioning, self-esteem, social connectedness, positive self-image, and psychological strengths.

Theme 2

Resilience as context-sensitive development

My work examines whether resilience has stable core features, which features change across cohorts or developmental stage, and how resilience should be measured in a changing social world.

Theme 3

Social cognition and self-perception

I examine mechanisms such as social comparison, perceived peer norms, intersubjectivity, self-concept clarity, identity, and status beliefs as links between social context and adolescent well-being.

Theme 4

Identity and digital environments

I study adolescent identity formation in hybrid social environments where digital and physical interactions are deeply connected, especially in relation to peer influence, belonging, and comparison.

Theme 5

Adolescent social realities

This line of research examines how adolescents experience and interpret contemporary social worlds, including peer cultures, media narratives, digital platforms, and public discourse about youth mental health.

Theme 6

Measurement and methods

I use psychometric and longitudinal methods to examine whether constructs such as resilience, well-being, and positive mental health remain stable across developmental and sociocultural contexts.

Conceptual frame

From individual deficit to social mechanism.

A recurring limitation in adolescent mental health research is the tendency to locate problems primarily inside the individual. My work asks how broader environments shape adolescents through social-cognitive processes.

Rather than treating context as background, I examine it as part of the developmental architecture of mental health. Adolescents' families, peers, schools, digital spaces, and cultures influence how they evaluate themselves and what they believe is possible.

Methods

Methodological approach

My research uses quantitative and mixed-methods approaches to study adolescent mental health as a dynamic and context-sensitive system.

  • Longitudinal survey designs
  • Experimental and quasi-experimental designs
  • Psychometrics and measurement invariance testing
  • Network analysis and systems approaches
  • Latent profile and person-centred methods
  • Social-cognitive and developmental models
  • Open-ended responses and qualitative coding where appropriate
